but the time loss is real.The same email should not be written from scratch every time.

Concept project AI implementation Jotform Zapier Claude API Gmail

Claire books around thirty sessions a month. After every booking, she writes a “here is what to expect” email. The structure is usually the same, but the names, locations, session types, and client details are different every time. She knows what she wants to say, but she spends too long finding the words.

This is not a chatbot. It is not a template with mail merge fields. It is a prompted AI workflow built around Claire’s own voice, so five minutes of input can become a draft she actually wants to send.

What happens next

Claire fills in five fields after a booking, and a draft that sounds like her appears in Gmail.

She reviews the email in a few minutes instead of writing it from a blank page.

Every client receives a message that feels personal, specific, and considered.

The AI never sends anything on its own. Claire reads the draft, adjusts it if needed, and sends it herself.

The stack

Four simple tools can return hours of writing time.

The tools are simple. The real work is in the prompt: documenting Claire’s voice, building the email structure, and making sure the draft sounds like something she would actually send.

Session intake

Jotform

Claire fills in five fields after confirming a booking. She adds the client names, session type, location, date, and a few notes from the inquiry call. That submission starts the workflow.

Connection layer

Zapier

Zapier receives the form submission and passes the details into the AI prompt. When the response comes back, Zapier creates a pre-filled Gmail draft in Claire’s inbox.

The writing

Claude API

The AI step uses the session details and Claire’s voice skeleton to create a full draft in her tone, her structure, and her sign-off. It gives her a strong starting point without making the email feel generic.

Review and send

Gmail

The draft lands in Claire’s Gmail. She reads it, adjusts anything she wants to change, and sends it. The final step stays with the person who owns the client relationship.

Why not use a template? A mail merge template can fill in names and dates, but the writing still sounds like a template. The AI step allows the sentence structure, tone, and details to change based on the session. A vineyard wedding in October should not read the same way as a beach family session in July.

Thirty sessions a month at thirty minutes of writing each is fifteen hours. Even if this tool cuts that in half, Claire gets meaningful time back every month. The API cost per draft is usually very small; the real work is in the setup, voice documentation, and prompt testing. For a business sending this many repeat-but-personal emails, the time savings can add up quickly.

Step 1 of 4

Zapier sends the variables to Claude, and Claude writes the draft.

Within seconds of Claire hitting submit, Zapier takes the form data and drops it into a prompt that already contains Claire’s voice skeleton. The prompt includes her tone, her structure, what she usually includes, and what she avoids saying.

Claude reads the variables, reads the skeleton, and produces a draft. This is the AI step. It is not a template with fields swapped in. It is a piece of writing that uses the session details the way Claire would use them herself.

Why this matters

The difference between a mail merge and this workflow is the difference between a generic reminder and an email that mentions Marc being camera shy, the dog, the anniversary, and golden hour at Reservoir Park. The AI reads the notes and writes from them, which is what a template cannot do.

Step 4 of 4

The voice skeleton is what makes the workflow useful.

The Zapier setup is the easier part. The API connection is straightforward once the workflow is mapped. The part that makes the output sound like Claire, instead of sounding like a generic AI draft, is the prompt.

We document how Claire writes. We capture what she usually includes, what she avoids saying, how she opens, how she closes, and how she handles the details that matter to each client. That becomes the skeleton the AI writes into every time.

Why this matters

Anyone can connect Jotform to Zapier to an AI model and get a generic result. The voice documentation is what makes the output usable. The value is not just the wiring. The value is in capturing how a specific person communicates clearly enough that the workflow can support her without flattening her voice.

What we built We built a documented voice guide for Claire, a structured prompt that uses it, and a Zapier workflow that passes session variables into it. The prompt on the right is a simplified version of what runs inside every API call.
claire-voice-prompt.txt — sent to Claude API on every call
# ROLE You are writing a pre-session email on behalf of Claire Leblanc, a portrait and family photographer based in the Annapolis Valley. Write in her voice, using the session variables and notes below.
# SESSION VARIABLES Client names: {{client_names}} Client email: {{client_email}} Session type: {{session_type}} Date: {{session_date}} Time: {{session_time}} Location: {{location}} Who is coming: {{who_is_coming}} Notes from call: {{call_notes}} Vibe: {{vibe}}
# CLAIRE’S VOICE - Claire writes warmly, but she does not gush. - She sounds conversational and calm. - She references specific details from the call. - She does not use corporate language. - She does not say “amazing,” “stunning,” “perfect,” or “journey.” - She signs off as “— Claire.” - She uses one clear logistics block and then moves on.
# EMAIL STRUCTURE 1. Open by name and reference something specific about the session. 2. Include a practical details block with the date, time, location, and access note. 3. Use one or two paragraphs to speak to the details from the call. 4. Mention gallery delivery and any relevant print notes. 5. Invite the client to reply with questions and sign off as Claire.
# OUTPUT Return the email body only. Do not include a subject line, preamble, or explanation.
