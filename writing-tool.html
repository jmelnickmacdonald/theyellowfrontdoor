What changed

Every client gets an email that feels personal, specific, and considered.

The AI does not send anything. Claire reads it, adjusts if she wants, and hits send herself.

Claire books around thirty sessions a month. After every booking she writes a “here’s what to expect” email. Same structure every time. Different names, locations, session types, and details every time. She knows what she wants to say. She just spends too long saying it.

This is not a chatbot. It is not a template with mail merge fields. It is a prompted AI workflow built around Claire’s own voice that takes five minutes of input and produces a draft she actually wants to send.

The stack

four tools, one less hour of writing.

The tools are simple. The work is in the prompt — documenting Claire’s voice, building the skeleton, and making sure what comes out the other end actually sounds like her.

Session intake

Jotform

Claire fills in five fields after confirming a booking. Client names, session type, location, date, and a few notes from the inquiry call. Thirty seconds. That submission is the trigger.

Connection layer

Zapier

Receives the form submission and passes the variables into the AI prompt. When the response comes back, Zapier creates a pre-filled Gmail draft in Claire’s inbox. She never leaves Gmail to make it work.

The writing

Claude API

The AI step. Zapier sends the session variables and Claire’s voice skeleton to the Claude API. What comes back is a full draft in her tone, her structure, and her sign-off. Not a template. Not a robot. A draft that sounds like her.

Review and send

Gmail

The draft lands in Claire’s Gmail. She reads it, adjusts anything she wants to, and sends. The AI never sends anything on its own. That part stays human.

Why not a template? A mail merge template fills in names and dates but the writing still sounds like a template. The AI step means the sentence structures vary, the tone matches the session vibe, and a vineyard wedding in October reads differently from a beach family session in July. That difference is the whole point.

Thirty sessions a month at thirty minutes of writing each is fifteen hours. Even if this tool cuts that in half, that is half a working week back every month. The API call itself costs fractions of a cent per email. The setup is a few hours of voice documentation and prompt work. It pays for itself in the first week.

The intake form front-loads the thinking Claire was going to do anyway. By the time she hits submit, the AI has everything it needs to write a specific, personal email. Garbage in, garbage out — the form makes sure what goes in is good.

Step 1 of 4

Within seconds of Claire hitting submit, Zapier takes the form data and drops it into a prompt that already contains Claire’s voice skeleton — her tone, her structure, what she always says and never says.

Claude reads the variables, reads the skeleton, and produces a draft. This is the AI step. Not a template with fields swapped in. A piece of writing that uses the session details the way Claire would use them herself.

Step 4 of 4

The voice skeleton is the real work. Everything else is plumbing.

The Zapier setup takes an afternoon. The Claude API connection is a dozen lines of configuration. The part that actually makes this work — that makes the output sound like Claire and not like a robot — is the prompt.

We document how Claire writes: what she always includes, what she never says, how she opens, how she closes, how she handles the details that matter to each client. That becomes the skeleton the AI writes into every time.

Why this matters

Anyone can connect Jotform to Zapier to Claude. The output will be generic. The voice documentation is what makes the output usable. That is the YFD work — not the wiring, but the thinking about how a specific person communicates, captured clearly enough that an AI can replicate it faithfully.

What we built A documented voice guide for Claire, a structured prompt that uses it, and a Zapier workflow that passes session variables into it. The prompt on the right is a simplified version of what runs inside every API call.
claire-voice-prompt.txt — sent to Claude API on every call
# ROLE You are writing a pre-session email on behalf of Claire Leblanc, a portrait and family photographer based in the Annapolis Valley. Write in her voice, using the session variables and notes below.
# SESSION VARIABLES (filled by Zapier from the form) Client names: {{client_names}} Client email: {{client_email}} Session type: {{session_type}} Date: {{session_date}} Time: {{session_time}} Location: {{location}} Who is coming: {{who_is_coming}} Notes from call: {{call_notes}} Vibe: {{vibe}}
# CLAIRE’S VOICE - Warm but not gushing. She is excited about the work, not performing excitement. - Conversational. Short sentences where short works. No corporate language. - Specific. She references details from the call. Generic reassurance is not her style. - She does not say “amazing”, “stunning”, “perfect”, or “journey”. - She signs off as “— Claire”. Never “Warmly” or “Best”. - She does not over-explain logistics. One clear block of details, then move on.
# EMAIL STRUCTURE 1. Open by name. One sentence that references something specific about this session. 2. Practical details block (date, time, location, parking or access note). 3. One or two paragraphs using the call notes. Address what matters to them specifically. 4. What to expect re: gallery delivery and any relevant print notes. 5. Invitation to reply with questions. Sign off as Claire.
# OUTPUT Return the email body only. No subject line. No preamble. No explanation. Just the email, ready to copy into Gmail.
