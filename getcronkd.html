Get Cronk'd Fitness Studio website, copy & systems.

A nine-year fitness studio with a world champion owner, a wall of awards, and a website that wasn't saying any of it. We rebuilt the whole thing — website, copy, and the operations system behind it — as one connected project.

The brief

Nine years of building something real. The website wasn't keeping up.

Rebecca Cronk opened Get Cronk'd in 2017. By 2025 she had a UFE Pro Wellness World Champion title, eight Diamond-level Readers' Choice Awards, a full roster of trainers, and a studio that had outlasted most gyms in the region by years.

The site was fine. It looked okay. But "fine" wasn't doing justice to nine years of work, a wall of awards, and a genuine community built class by class. The words were careful and generic. The design was serviceable. Nothing on it felt like walking into the actual studio.

The brief underneath the brief: make it feel like that.

Behind the decisions

Leading with nine years, not a tagline.

Most fitness studio sites lead with transformation language. Before and after photos. Motivational copy. Generic promises about reaching your goals. We didn't want any of that.

What Rebecca had built over nine years was specific, earned, and hard to fake. A private 2,000 square foot facility. A world champion coaching your first squat. A 6am crew that keeps showing up because the studio genuinely earned their loyalty.

That's what the site needed to say. Not "get fit." Not "we're here to help you reach your goals." The copy leads with longevity because nine years in this industry is the real thing worth saying — it's confident without being loud, and it makes you curious before you've read anything else.

"There are plenty of places to work out. Here's why people drive past all of them to come here."

The visitor path cards were a deliberate structural choice too. Instead of asking someone to navigate a services page, the site meets them where they actually are: "I want a personal trainer." "I haven't worked out in a long time." "I'm looking for something for my child." Six entry points, each written for the real person standing in front of it.
