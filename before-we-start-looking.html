Working Together

What it looks like to work with me, how we will communicate, and what to expect from the first conversation.

In this section

  • How the first conversation works.
  • What it means to work with a buyer’s agent.
  • What I will help you understand before we start looking seriously.
  • How to add a note or question for our next conversation.

The main idea

You do not need to be fully ready before you reach out.

The first conversation is a place to get oriented. We can talk through where you are, what you are hoping for, and what needs to happen before the search becomes serious.

Add a Note

Starting point

The first conversation should feel easy.

When you reach out, you do not need to have your budget perfected, your neighbourhood narrowed down, or your full wish list ready. It is completely normal to begin with a mix of ideas, questions, and uncertainty.

Our first conversation is a chance to slow things down and sort through the basics. We can talk about what you are hoping to do, what timeline feels realistic, what you already know, and what still feels unclear.

A helpful way to think about it: you are not contacting me because you have all the answers. You are contacting me so we can figure out the right next steps together.

Representation

What it means to work with a buyer’s agent.

In Nova Scotia, a buyer can choose to work with a brokerage as a client. When that happens, the relationship and expectations are documented through a buyer brokerage agreement. The goal is to make the working relationship clear, including responsibilities, representation, timing, and compensation.

That agreement is not meant to make the process feel intimidating. It is there so you understand who is representing you, what duties are owed to you, and how the professional relationship works before we get too far into the search.

What I can help with

  • Searching for homes that match your goals, budget, and timeline.
  • Booking and attending showings with you.
  • Helping you understand what to look for in a property.
  • Walking through offer terms, conditions, timelines, and next steps.
  • Helping you know when to bring in other professionals, such as a lender, lawyer, insurer, inspector, or other specialist.

I can help you understand the real estate process, but I am not a replacement for legal, mortgage, insurance, tax, or inspection advice. When something needs that expertise, I will help you know who should be part of the conversation.

What to expect

How we will usually begin.

Every buyer is different, but the early process often starts with a few simple pieces: understanding your timeline, checking in on financing, clarifying what matters, and deciding when it makes sense to start viewing homes.

1. We talk through where you are. Maybe you are early, maybe you are ready, or maybe you are not sure. That helps us decide what to do first.
2. We look at money and timing. We will talk generally about pre-approval, comfort level, deposit, closing costs, and what needs to be confirmed.
3. We narrow the search. We will sort must-haves from nice-to-haves and look at what your budget and areas are realistically giving you.
4. We start viewing with a plan. Once we are ready, showings become more useful because we know what we are looking for and what questions to ask.

Communication

Good communication makes this easier.

Buying a home can move from slow to very fast. Clear communication helps us both stay on the same page. If something changes with your budget, timeline, areas, or comfort level, it is always better to say so early.

You can also tell me how you like to communicate. Some buyers prefer quick texts. Some want email summaries. Some need a call when the details get more serious. We can find the rhythm that works.

  • Text is useful for quick updates and simple questions.
  • Email is better for longer notes, documents, and summaries.
  • Phone calls are helpful when a decision needs context or there are multiple moving pieces.
