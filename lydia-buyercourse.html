Buyer Guide

The Nuts and Bolts of Buying a Home

A practical guide to help you understand the process, get organized, and feel more prepared before the search gets busy.

A note from Lydia

Buying a home comes with a lot of moving parts. Some buyers are just starting out. Some have bought before but need a refresher. Some know exactly what they want, and some are still figuring that out.

This guide gives you a clear place to begin. It is not homework, and you do not need to have everything figured out before we talk. Use it to understand the basics, sort your thoughts, and send me the details that will help me guide you well.

Module 01

Where are you in the process?

There is no perfect place to start. You may be curious, nervous, very ready, or somewhere in the middle. The first step is simply getting clear on where you are right now.

In Nova Scotia, if you decide to work with a REALTOR® as a client, you should expect the relationship and expectations to be put in writing through a brokerage agreement. This helps clarify representation, duties, and compensation from the start.

  • Tell me what you already know.
  • Share what feels unclear or overwhelming.
  • Let me know whether you are exploring, watching listings, or ready to book showings.

Module 02

What do you need to understand before viewing?

Your lender or mortgage broker will help you understand what you may be approved for, what the monthly payment could look like, and what to expect for closing costs. The approved number is not always the same as the number that feels comfortable.

In Nova Scotia, buyers should also plan for legal fees, adjustments, insurance, inspection costs, moving costs, and municipal deed transfer tax where applicable. Municipal deed transfer tax is set by each municipality and collected when the deed is registered.

  • Know whether you have started pre-approval.
  • Think about monthly comfort, not only the purchase price.
  • Ask early about deposits, closing costs, deed transfer tax, and legal fees.

Module 03

What does the home need to do for your life?

A useful search is not just a list of features. It is about how you live, what you value, and what will make the home work after the excitement of the showing wears off.

Bedrooms and bathrooms matter, but so do storage, winter access, commute, school routes, yard work, road noise, heating type, internet availability, pets, guests, and maintenance.

  • Separate must-haves from nice-to-haves.
  • Be honest about dealbreakers.
  • Think about daily life, not just the listing description.

Module 04

What should you pay attention to during a showing?

Photos can make a home look simple. Walking through it tells you more. This section helps you stay practical while still paying attention to how the home feels.

The Nova Scotia Real Estate Commission strongly recommends having a home inspection. A showing is not a replacement for an inspection, but it is a good time to notice what you may want to ask about later.

  • How does the layout work for your daily routines?
  • Is there enough storage, parking, and practical space?
  • What looks well cared for, and what might need attention?
  • How does the street, driveway, yard, or building feel?

Module 05

What felt right, and what gave you pause?

It is easy for showings to blur together. A quick reflection helps us keep track of what actually worked, what did not, and what we should look into before you get too attached.

This is where we separate excitement from fit. A home can feel charming and still be wrong for your life. A home can also feel imperfect but be worth a second look if the important pieces are there.

  • Write down your reaction while it is fresh.
  • Separate excitement from actual fit.
  • Flag questions before they get lost.

Module 06

Are you clear enough to move forward?

When a home feels like the one, things can move quickly. In Nova Scotia, the Agreement of Purchase and Sale is a legally binding contract once accepted, subject to the terms and conditions written into it.

Common buyer conditions may include financing, insurance, inspection, review of documents, sale of a current home, or other property-specific due diligence. If buyer conditions are satisfied, Nova Scotia uses a buyer waiver of conditions process to confirm they have been waived by the deadline.

  • Comfort with price and monthly cost.
  • Deposit and closing timeline.
  • Financing, inspection, insurance, legal, and property-specific conditions.
  • What still needs to be confirmed before you feel confident.

Module 6 of 6

A good process gives you room to think.

Buying a home involves practical decisions and emotional ones. The point of this guide is not to slow you down for the sake of it. It is to help you notice what matters before the process gets busy.

When we understand the budget, the must-haves, the dealbreakers, and the questions early, our conversations are better. We can spend less time guessing and more time making thoughtful decisions.
