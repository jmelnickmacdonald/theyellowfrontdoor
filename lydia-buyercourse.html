Buyer Guide

The Nuts and Bolts of Buying a Home

A practical guide to help you understand the process, get organized, and feel more prepared before the search gets busy.

A note from Lydia

Buying a home comes with a lot of moving parts. Some buyers are just starting out. Some have bought before but need a refresher. Some know exactly what they want, and some are still figuring that out.

This guide gives you a clear place to begin. It is not meant to be homework, and you do not need to have everything figured out before we talk. Use it to understand the basics, sort your thoughts, and send me the details that will help me guide you well.

Buyer checkpoints

Short, useful questions at the right time.

You can move through the whole guide or jump to the part that matches where you are right now.

Start Here

Where are you in the process?

Everyone starts in a different place. You might be ready to view homes this week, or you might just be trying to understand what buying could look like. Both are completely fine.

  • Tell me what you already know.
  • Share what feels unclear or overwhelming.
  • Let me know how soon you hope to move.

Start here

This gives me a better sense of where to begin, so our first conversation is useful instead of overwhelming.

Money + Readiness

What do you need to understand before viewing?

You do not need every financial detail perfect before we talk, but it helps to know what still needs to be sorted out. Budget, deposit, pre-approval, monthly comfort, and closing costs all matter.

  • Know whether you have spoken with a lender or mortgage broker.
  • Think about what monthly payment feels comfortable, not just what you may be approved for.
  • Flag questions about deposits, closing costs, or conditions.

Money + readiness

This does not replace advice from your lender, broker, lawyer, or accountant. It helps me understand where you may need more guidance before we move quickly.

What You’re Looking For

What does the home need to do for your life?

A useful search is not just a list of features. It is about how you live, what you value, and what will make the home work after the excitement of the showing wears off.

  • Separate must-haves from nice-to-haves.
  • Be honest about dealbreakers.
  • Think about daily life, not just the listing description.

Your priorities

A good showing is not only about whether you like the house.

It is also about noticing what you might be taking on. Layout, light, storage, heating, water, road noise, stairs, parking, yard maintenance, future repairs, and the feel of the street all matter.

You do not need to inspect the home yourself. That is what qualified professionals are for. But you can learn to notice what deserves a closer look.

Before You View

What should you pay attention to during a showing?

Photos can make a home look simple. Walking through it tells you more. This section helps you stay practical while still paying attention to how the home feels.

  • How does the layout work for your daily routines?
  • Is there enough storage, parking, and practical space?
  • What looks well cared for, and what might need attention?
  • How does the street, driveway, yard, or building feel?

After a Showing

What felt right, and what gave you pause?

It is easy for showings to blur together. A quick reflection helps us keep track of what actually worked, what did not, and what we should look into before you get too attached.

  • Write down your reaction while it is fresh.
  • Separate excitement from actual fit.
  • Flag questions before they get lost.

After a showing

Before You Write an Offer

Are you clear enough to move forward?

When a home feels like the one, things can move quickly. This check-in helps us slow down just enough to make sure you understand the major pieces before we talk about an offer.

  • Comfort with price and monthly cost.
  • Deposit and closing timeline.
  • Financing, inspection, insurance, legal, and any property-specific conditions.
  • What still needs to be confirmed before you feel confident.

Offer readiness

You are not expected to know every detail. My job is to walk you through the pieces, explain what matters, and help you make a decision you understand.

What I receive

A clearer picture of what you need, without a long questionnaire.

When you complete a checkpoint, I receive a simple summary of your priorities, questions, and comfort level. That means I can guide you with better context and spend less time guessing what matters most.

It does not replace our conversations. It makes them better.
