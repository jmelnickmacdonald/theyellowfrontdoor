A practical guide to help you understand the process, get organized, and feel more prepared as we start looking together.

Section 01

Working Together

The first step is not rushing into houses. It is making sure we both understand what you need, what you are hoping for, and how the process will work.

In Nova Scotia, if you choose to work with a brokerage as a client, that relationship is normally documented through a buyer brokerage agreement. The point is to make the relationship clearer: who is representing you, what duties are owed, how long the agreement lasts, and how compensation is handled.

  • We talk through where you are in the process.
  • We clarify what you need from me and what I can help with.
  • We make sure representation and expectations are understood before the search gets serious.

Section 02

Before We Start Looking

Before we book a lot of showings, it helps to understand the practical pieces: budget, pre-approval, deposit, closing costs, legal fees, insurance, and timing.

In Nova Scotia, buyers should also be aware of deed transfer tax. It is set by each municipality and collected when the deed is registered, so it is worth asking about early instead of being surprised later.

  • Talk to a lender or mortgage broker about pre-approval.
  • Think about monthly comfort, not only purchase price.
  • Ask about deposit, legal fees, deed transfer tax, insurance, inspection costs, and adjustments.

Section 03

Pin Down What Matters

A useful search is not just a list of features. It is about how you live, what you value, and what will make the home work after the excitement of a showing wears off.

Bedrooms and bathrooms matter, but so do storage, winter access, commute, school routes, yard work, road noise, heating type, internet availability, pets, guests, and how much maintenance you actually want.

  • Separate must-haves from nice-to-haves.
  • Be honest about dealbreakers.
  • Think about daily life, not just the listing description.

Section 04

Showing Tour Day

Showings can be exciting, but they can also blur together. The goal is to look at the home with both sides of your brain: how it feels, and how it functions.

The Nova Scotia Real Estate Commission strongly recommends having a home inspection. A showing is not a replacement for an inspection, but it is a good time to notice what we may want to ask about later.

  • Notice layout, storage, light, parking, access, and daily-life flow.
  • Look beyond furniture, paint colours, and staging.
  • Make note of visible repairs, systems, yard condition, and location questions.

Section 05

After a Showing

It is easy for showings to blur together. A quick reflection helps us keep track of what actually worked, what did not, and what we should look into before you get too attached.

This is where we separate excitement from fit. A home can feel charming and still be wrong for your life. A home can also feel imperfect but be worth a second look if the important pieces are there.

  • Write down your reaction while it is fresh.
  • Separate excitement from actual fit.
  • Flag questions before they get lost.

Section 06

Thinking About an Offer

When a home feels like the one, things can move quickly. If the seller accepts an offer, the Agreement of Purchase and Sale becomes the purchase contract, subject to the terms and conditions written into it.

Common buyer conditions may include financing, inspection, insurance, review of documents, sale of a current home, or other property-specific due diligence. In Nova Scotia, buyer conditions are explicitly waived using Form 408 by the condition deadline.

  • Comfort with price and monthly cost.
  • Deposit and closing timeline.
  • Financing, inspection, insurance, legal, and any property-specific conditions.
  • What still needs to be confirmed before you feel confident.
