How we work on websites

Some clients come to us with a site already — on Squarespace, WordPress, whatever — and just need it to work better. We're comfortable in most platforms and happy to work within what you've already got.

When we build from scratch, there are two ways we approach it. Some clients want a completely custom site that looks exactly right and stays that way — the design is locked, and we handle any changes that come up. Others want that same level of custom work but with the ability to update their own content: swap a photo, edit a price, add a class to the schedule. Every site we build in that second camp comes with a purpose-built dashboard that makes those changes simple, without any risk of touching the design.

The projects below are a mix of all three.

Fin & Oak — Behind the decisions

01 — The hero photos

Most pet food brands lead with the product. We led with Oak and Piper because the whole point of Fin & Oak is who's behind it and who it's for. You should feel that before you read a single word.

02 — The headline

You want your website to feel like something the second you land on it. "Small batches. Big messes. Happy critters." does that. It tells you exactly what it's like in this kitchen before you've scrolled, clicked, or read anything else.

03 — The subhead copy

"The rest of the menagerie" is the phrase you use when you've genuinely stopped counting. Two dogs, six cats, chickens with strong opinions, geese who don't like anyone. This is a real house. The copy should sound like it.

04 — "Real ingredients. Spoiled regulars."

What's in it, and is it worth it. Two questions, six words. "Spoiled regulars" also does something quietly useful: it tells you people come back. No testimonials needed.

05 — Willow & Mom

Three words that tell the whole story. You know exactly who made this before you've read anything else on the page. That's the whole point of signing off with names.

06 — The logo

We needed something that could live on a website, stamp onto a bag, and shrink down to a sticker without falling apart. Playfair gave us the presence and polish without tipping into cute territory, which was the line we were trying not to cross. "Bark Bakery" tracked out underneath grounds it and makes the whole mark stamp cleanly. No icon needed. The type does the work.

Anchored Prose — Behind the decisions

01 — The hero image

I live in Nova Scotia and I write by the water whenever I can, so it made no sense to use a stock image of a desk or a coffee cup when the place that actually inspires my work looks like this. The hero image isn't decoration. It's context.

02 — The headline

"Every word, anchored in purpose" is the whole philosophy of this business in five words. I don't do filler, I don't write around a point or pad a paragraph to make something feel weightier than it is, and every word on this site is there because it earns its place. The headline isn't a tagline. It's a promise.

03 — The frosted overlay panel

I didn't want the landscape to disappear behind the words, so the frosted panel lets the copy sit clearly without swallowing the water entirely. The setting belongs on this page as much as anything I've written on it.

04 — Captain's Log

This is my ship and the navigation language runs through the whole site because it means something, not because it's a cute theme. Captain's Log is my blog, still finding its sea legs, but the name was never going to be anything else.

05 — The colour palette

These are my colours, the ones I reach for because they feel true to me. Serene, coastal, a little moody at the edges, they didn't come from a brand brief or a trend report but from paying close attention to where I live and what the water looks like at different hours of the day.

Get Cronk'd — Behind the decisions

01 — The brief underneath the brief

Rebecca knew her site needed work. What came through in the first conversation was that it didn't sound like her — it was a little careful, a little distant. The real studio is warm and a bit chaotic in the best way. The brief was just: make it feel like that.

02 — Leading with nine years

Most fitness studio sites lead with transformation language. We led with longevity because nine years in this industry is the real thing worth saying. It's confident without being loud, and it makes you curious before you've read anything else.

03 — The awards

Eight Diamond categories is a lot, and we put them on the page because they belong there. But we didn't build the whole site around them — the people who come to Get Cronk'd don't come because of an award. They come because someone they trust told them it was different here. The awards confirm it. They don't start it.

04 — The editing dashboard

Rebecca updates her class schedule regularly. Testimonials come in. Awards get added. With most custom sites, every one of those changes means calling someone. We built her a dashboard that looks like a simple form — she opens it, makes the change, saves it. The design doesn't move. Nothing breaks. That's how every site we build works.
