Getting found is not the same as being chosen.

Most marketing investment goes into visibility — ads, posts, reach, more traffic, more eyes. That work matters. But visibility only pays off if the experience people land in is clear enough, warm enough, and current enough to hold their attention.

The Yellow Front Door works on that experience. The website. The replies. The voice. The follow-up. The welcome email. The Google profile that loads before you do. The half of marketing that decides whether the rest of it was worth spending.

We start with the foundation. Once it's solid, more visibility makes sense — and we're happy to help with that too. But foundation first.

01

The First Look

Voice, listings, and first impressions

Do you sound like yourself before anyone meets you?

Best for

Your business feels scattered before people even reach your website — different surfaces, different voices, none of it quite matching who you actually are.

What it does

Defines how your business sounds and rewrites every place it shows up in the world to match. Voice and presence, made consistent.

Can include

Voice and tone guide, Google Business Profile, social bios, directory listings, taglines, email signature, short descriptions.

Natural next step

Often leads into The Window or The Touch-Up.

02

The Window

Website, landing pages, and the path in

Does it invite people in?

Best for

People find you, but the website doesn't quite help them understand, trust, or take the next step.

What it does

Clarifies the digital storefront and the path from interest to action. Makes it easier for the right people to say yes.

Can include

Website builds or refreshes, landing pages, contact forms, page copy, calls to action, search visibility basics.

Natural next step

Often pairs with The Welcome Mat.

03

The Welcome Mat

First replies, welcome emails, and early follow-up

Does the first moment feel right?

Best for

People inquire, sign up, or book — but the first thing they hear back feels thin, cold, or like it came from a different business entirely.

What it does

Shapes the first 24 to 48 hours after someone arrives. The impression that sets the tone for everything that follows.

Can include

Welcome emails, inquiry replies, onboarding flows, auto-responders, confirmation messages, contact form follow-ups.

Natural next step

Pairs well with The Window and The Open Door.

04

The Open Door

Newsletters, content, and ongoing relationship

Are you still showing up after the welcome?

Best for

You have past customers, warm leads, or interested people who already know you — but no steady rhythm for staying connected with them.

What it does

Builds the inside-the-relationship rhythms that keep the business present long after the first hello — for people who have already come in.

Can include

Newsletters, customer emails, content systems, retention sequences, recurring communication, light campaign planning.

Natural next step

Natural bridge into The Touch-Up.

05

The Touch-Up

Monthly continuity and upkeep

Is anyone keeping it sharp?

Best for

Things have been built, but no one is keeping them current, aligned, or reflective of the business as it continues to grow and change.

What it does

Keeps the in-market experience from quietly drifting out of date. Maintains what has been built — light touch through full care.

Can include

Copy refreshes, small website edits, seasonal updates, voice checks, listing updates, offer updates, regular review.

Natural next step

The primary ongoing offer. Usually follows any build engagement.

About

Julianna
Melnick-MacDonald

For about a decade, I ran a retail and custom sign business — workshops, products, custom orders. Sales were good, customers were happy, and I was teaching full time and raising kids at the same time, which meant any given Tuesday involved approximately seventeen things happening at once and none of them going according to plan.

The business worked. The infrastructure around it was another story.

My website was perpetually out of date. Booking was more convoluted than it needed to be. I was paying for Facebook ads, radio spots, and every local visibility opportunity that crossed my desk, and honestly, my train was moving so fast I couldn't have told you whether any of it was converting. I was writing individual replies to every inquiry because building a template was always next week's job. I was doing three times the work I should have been and never getting ahead of the curve, because the friction was invisible to me until I was deep enough in to see it clearly.

Nobody told me that half the hustle was just friction.

COVID reshuffled things — as it did for most of us. My family moved across the country, I stepped away from the classroom, and spent some time figuring out how best to put my actual skills to use. Turns out the thread running through all of it — teaching, running a business, writing, designing — was the same thing I'd been doing in different clothes for years: taking complicated, messy things and making them clear.

The Yellow Front Door is the studio I wish had existed when I was the one with the outdated website and the marketing budget I couldn't account for. It's built on a very specific understanding: that small business owners aren't failing at this because they don't care. They're failing because they're busy running an actual business, and the customer-facing infrastructure quietly falls behind while they do.

I work with a small number of clients at a time and I'll be straight with you about what I see. I won't sell you visibility work if your foundation isn't ready for it, and I'll tell you if the thing you think needs fixing isn't actually the thing that needs fixing.

If your business is doing good work and the outside isn't keeping up with the inside, that's exactly the conversation I'm built for.
