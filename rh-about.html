Our story

We moved to a 200-year-old house
with no idea what we were doing.
That part hasn't changed.

In 2023, we found ourselves owners of a nearly 200-year-old home. It felt less like moving in and more like stepping into a living history.

We moved to the Annapolis Valley, just outside Greenwood, where the Black River winds its way through. The house had creaking floors, deep roots, and stories tucked into every corner. Six of us. No farm experience to speak of.

We traded the commute for birdsong and noisy roosters, river walks, and the everyday rhythm of a life lived differently. We gained a slower pace and an appreciation for small things. Mushroom fairy circles under decades-old spruce. The way the river rises and falls as the year turns.

We are raising our kids to notice things. To do the actual work. To be outside more than inside. The farm has been the best teacher we've found.

"We are raising our children to notice, to wonder, and to take part in the crazy we call life."
— Raven House Co.
The name

Two ravens were waiting in the driveway on moving day.

Pulling into the driveway on moving day, there stood two ravens, as if waiting. Not long after, we realized they were here before us and weren't going anywhere.

Even now, they circle above us several times a day, keeping watch, and happily accepting eggs when offered. We named the place after them. It felt right.

What we believe

A few things we've learned since moving here.

The animals don't care what's on the calendar

Feed them anyway. That rhythm has done more for this family than any routine we've tried to build.

The farm keeps you on your toes

Every day is different. A late frost, a fence that needs fixing before dark, an egg that didn't make it. The kids are learning things out here that no curriculum could hand them.

We show the real version

This life is loud and muddy, and we share it that way. If it looks like work, that's because it is.

Eggs on a doorstep still happens here

A borrowed trailer, a neighbour who shows up with a chainsaw after a storm. People in this valley still do that, and it has changed how we think about community.

You can tell when someone cared about making it

It doesn't need to be precious, but there is a difference between something made with attention and something that came off a line.

The farm keeps giving you things to notice

A new calf, the first egg of spring, a raven on the fence post. Slow down enough and this place just keeps handing you something worth seeing.
