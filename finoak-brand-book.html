A note to start

Before you go any further.

This is the whole brand in one place. If it isn't in here, we haven't decided it yet — so go ahead and ask, don't guess.

Here's the thing about a small brand: it drifts if you let it. The blue creeps a few shades off. Someone writes "we are thrilled to announce" and means well, and suddenly we sound like a phone company. The logo ends up squished onto a lime-green background at a craft fair. None of that is anyone's fault — it's just what happens when the rules live in one person's head instead of on a page.

So I wrote them down. Everything in here we chose on purpose, and where there's a rule there's a reason sitting right next to it. Follow it and the whole thing stays of a piece — the website, the bags, the farmers' market table, a thank-you note three Christmases from now. They should all feel like they came from the same two people in the same messy kitchen. Because they did.

xo, Willow & Julianna

The story.

01 · The story

How a kitchen-counter idea became a bakery.

Most good ideas in this family start at the kitchen counter. This one took a few years to leave it.

Willow and I have been talking about a pet treat business since she was six. We'd float a name, sketch a logo on the back of something, get properly serious for an evening — and then dinner would happen. Or homework. Or it'd be bedtime, or a laptop would die mid-sentence, or life would just keep on lifeing. The whole thing got shelved until the next round. For about five years that was the pattern.

Well, life be darned — it's time. We're doing it now, mostly because at eleven Willow is genuinely awesome and self-sufficient and I can trust her not to lose a finger to the stand mixer without me in the room. (Just don't hand the kid a grapefruit knife. That's a story for another day.) This is officially her gig. I'm her partner on the build — on oven timers, on anything that needs a credit card, and on the bits that take grown-up expertise. Don't tell her that last part; she'll say otherwise.

Where the name came from

Fin was our first dog — the one we brought every single kid home to. He'd appointed himself canine household manager long before Willow showed up, and he loved her exactly the way she loved him: patiently, steadily, always nearby. He passed this spring, just shy of thirteen. He epitomized what a great dog life looked like. And what a great dog looked like, while he was at it. He set a high standard here, I tell you.

Oak came along a few years after Fin and learned the whole job from him. He's the easiest creature in the house — lives to sunbathe on the deck, lets the chaos roll past him, has never once had a complicated day. We named the bakery for both of them. They're the dogs who taught this family what a dog could be.

Who's actually here

Two dogs, six cats, a few chickens with strong opinions, and the geese — who, for the record, don't like anyone. Oak is still on duty, mostly by being present. Piper is the youngest, scared of her own shadow until somebody looks sideways at one of the kids; she's Willow's girl through and through. She came along after the name was already settled.

One rule that matters everywhere: Fin is honoured, not counted. When we talk about the taste-testers, the living dogs are two — Oak and Piper. Fin lives in the story and the panorama. He never goes in the count.

The bits a stranger needs to get right: the voice is mine (Julianna), writing with and for Willow, who's eleven. We open July 2026. We're in Meadowvale, in the Annapolis Valley, Nova Scotia. Home base is Raven House. We sign off xo, Willow & Julianna. The site doubles as a portfolio piece for my marketing shop, The Yellow Front Door — so it has to be good.

02 · The logo

The wordmark.

"Fin & Oak" in Playfair, with "Bark Bakery" tracked out underneath. The type is the whole identity — there's no little icon off to the side doing the heavy lifting.

Please do

Give it room to breathe — clear space all the way around equal to the height of the ampersand. Set it on cream, on Coastal Blue, or on a calm photo. Lead with the wide version; only reach for the stacked one when you're truly tight on width.

Please don't

Don't recolour it outside the palette, stretch or squish it, drop a shadow behind it, or sit it on a busy photo where the name disappears. And don't rebuild that ampersand in some other font — the italic & is part of the mark, not a stand-in.

The files, for whoever's asking: the wordmark is finandoakbarkbakeryfinal.png. The hand-signed xo, Willow & Julianna (xowillowandjulianna.png) and the madebyhand.png footer line are companions to the logo — lovely, but not replacements for it.

03 · The colours

The palette.

Four colours do all the work. Everything else on this page is just a quieter version of one of them.

#074F6D
Coastal Blue
RGB 7 / 79 / 109

Our colour. The one people should picture when they think of us. Big moments, the wordmark, the "tell me when" buttons. Calm and sure of itself on cream.

#8B6F47
Caramel
RGB 139 / 111 / 71

The warm one. Little labels, hairline rules, a touch of emphasis. It reads a bit vintage, a bit kitchen. Never let it run a whole wall.

#FFFDF9
Cream
RGB 255 / 253 / 249

Where everything sits. Not white — white feels like a dentist's office, and that is not what we do. This is the paper everything's printed on.

#1A1A1A
Soft Ink
RGB 26 / 26 / 26

The words. Body copy, headlines, the ears. A hair warmer than true black, because true black is cold. Don't swap in #000 — you'll feel the difference even if you can't name it.

#A6B088
Apple Leaf
RGB 166 / 176 / 136

The sage off the apple trees we kept tripping over when we first walked the Raven House property. A background, and only ever a background. Not text, not the logo, not a button.

The 60–30–10 rule

Roughly 60% cream, 30% ink and empty space, and 10% blue-and-caramel between them. The accents only work while they stay accents — a page where everything's blue is a page where nothing is. And Apple Leaf never gets more room than blue and caramel put together. It's the supporting cast, not a fourth lead.

Defaults, not laws — if the room calls for something else, the room wins.

For whoever's in the code: the variables are --font-display (Playfair) and --font-body (Lato) — not the --font-playfair / --font-lato you might guess. Eyebrows and small labels get 0.20–0.28em of tracking. That little bit of breathing room is what makes uppercase look editorial instead of shouty.

05.

The voice.

Warm, dry, specific, honest. Like a person you'd actually want to talk to.

05 · The voice

How we sound.

Here's the test: if a marketing department could've written it, rewrite it. If it sounds like a specific person standing in a specific kitchen, you've got it.

Be specific, not nice.

"The geese don't like anyone" beats "all our animals get along" every time. The detail is the proof we're telling the truth. Anyone can say nice. Only we can say the geese.

Keep it dry.

The joke lives in the aside, in the parenthesis, in the thing we almost didn't say. Never zany, never a punchline reaching for a laugh. A raised eyebrow, not a clown nose.

Talk to one person.

"Tell your dog we said hi." Second person, one reader, a real human on the other end of it — not "valued customers," not "pet parents everywhere."

Sound like where we're from.

Flavours, neighbours, apples from down the road, snow on the deck. Canadian and unbothered about it. Local beats generic, always.

Say the true thing, even when it's not finished.

"Finalized recipes closer to launch." "Still refining this one, because… cats." We'd rather be honest and a little unready than polished and vague.

This is us

"Small batches. Big messes. Happy critters."

"You'll know what's in it."

"Tested by a thorough and committed team of testers."

"Made bigger, for bigger appetites."

"Tell your dog we said hi."

This is never us

"We are thrilled to announce…"

"Delicious treats made with love."

"Your furry friend deserves the very best!"

"Premium · all-natural · vet-approved."

"Link in bio!!"

The unglamorous rules that still matter: straight quotes only in anything headed for the code — the curly kind quietly break things. The email is hello@finandoak.ca, everywhere, no exceptions. And the footer says it plainly: Powered by The Yellow Front Door · a Raven House venture.

06 · The ear cast

The cast.

A set of little ear silhouettes that runs through everything — dividing sections, marking lists, signing off a footer. Small, but unmistakably us.

They're vector SVGs in Soft Ink, living at /public/ears/ear-XX.svg, and they scale from a bullet point to a whole wall without ever going fuzzy. On the Story page they already do this work. On the Home and Treats pages, the three wax-seal stamps — dog, cat, horse — carry the category jobs instead.

All fifteen, traced from hand-drawn originals. Each one is a different head in this house.

How to treat them

The rotation ruleNever the same ear twice in a row on one page. The variety is the whole charm — repeat one and it stops being a cast and starts being a logo.
ColourSoft Ink, full stop. Don't tint them blue or caramel or sage.
Size16px as a bullet, all the way up to a full-page illustration. Keep the proportions locked.
NamesThe filenames are stable. Don't rename ear-XX.svg without telling the design system, or things go quietly missing.

Real ingredients. Spoiled regulars. Made by hand, in a real kitchen, by a mom and a daughter who'd happily eat it themselves — and, on at least one occasion, have.
