The Cake Co.order in, automation on.

A custom cake studio was losing customers in the silence between form submission and follow-up. We built a four-email automated sequence that handles the whole order lifecycle, coded in HTML and loaded directly into MailerLite.

Concept project Order automation Email sequence MailerLite Square

The Cake Co. is a fictional custom cake studio built as a sample scenario. The tools, the copy, and the flow are illustrative. If you take custom orders, yours would be shaped around how you actually work.

The scenario: Sarah finds The Cake Co. online on a Friday night. She fills out a custom order form for her daughter's 40th birthday cake. What happens next is the whole point.

The stack

four tools, one clean flow.

This is a suggested stack for a business at this size and price point. The tools are accessible, the costs are reasonable, and none of it requires a developer to maintain.

Order intake

Jotform

Custom order form captures occasion, servings, flavour, delivery details, and design notes. All of it feeds directly into the automation.

Connection layer

Zapier

When a form is submitted, Zapier adds the contact to MailerLite and triggers the sequence. One connection, set up once.

Email automation

MailerLite

Coded HTML templates loaded directly into MailerLite. The automation runs the sequence, times the sends, and handles the logic.

Payment

Square

The quote email includes a Square invoice link. Customer pays the deposit, Square sends their receipt, date is locked.

Nothing here is fixed. The tools, the timing, the number of emails, the payment flow, all of it can shift to fit how your business actually runs. This is a starting point, not a prescription.

Walk through the sequence

what Sarah experiences, step by step.

Click through each step below to see exactly what happens after Sarah hits submit. What she receives, when she receives it, and why each email is doing what it is doing.

Step 1 of 4

Sarah submits her order.

It is Friday evening. Sarah finds The Cake Co. on Instagram, clicks through to the website, and fills out the custom order form. Within seconds, this email lands in her inbox.

She did not have to wait for anyone to be awake or at their phone. The automation picked it up the moment she hit submit.

Why this matters

Most custom order businesses go quiet after a form submission. No acknowledgement, no confirmation, nothing. Sarah does not know if it went through. This email closes that gap immediately and plays her order details back to her so she feels seen before anyone has even looked at her request.

Step 2 of 4

The baker reviews the order and sends the quote.

Saturday morning, the baker looks at Sarah’s order. Pricing a custom cake takes a human eye. Size, flavour, design complexity, and delivery distance all factor in. This is the one step in the sequence that is not automated.

She builds the quote in Square, copies the invoice link, and sends this email through MailerLite. Sarah gets her pricing, a line-item receipt, and a direct link to pay the deposit, all in one email.

Why this matters

The quote email does two things at once. It is a warm, branded follow-up that keeps Sarah engaged, and it is a functional payment request. The Square invoice link means she can pay immediately without any back-and-forth. One email, no friction.

Step 3 of 4

Sarah pays the deposit. Her date is locked.

Sarah clicks the Square link, pays $155, and Square sends her a payment receipt automatically. The baker is notified by Square the moment it clears.

This confirmation email goes out once the deposit is confirmed, either triggered automatically via Zapier or sent manually by the baker when she sees the payment come through.

Option A

Zapier watches Square

Deposit clears, Zapier fires, email sends automatically within minutes. Around $20/month extra.

Higher volume businesses.

Option B

One manual trigger

Baker sees the Square notification, triggers the email manually in MailerLite. Takes 30 seconds. No extra cost.

Best for lower volume.

Step 4 of 4

48 hours before delivery, Sarah hears from them again.

This email goes out automatically two days before the confirmed delivery date. The baker did not have to remember to send it. MailerLite fires it based on the delivery date captured in the original form.

Sarah knows exactly what to expect. The delivery window, the balance owing, how to pay, and what to do to be ready. No calls, no confusion, no last-minute scramble.

Why this matters

Custom delivery has a lot of moving parts. A reminder 48 hours out means Sarah is prepared, the baker does not have to chase anyone for final payment, and nothing falls through the cracks on the day itself. The customer feels looked after. The baker stays focused on the cake.
